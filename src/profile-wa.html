Position Profile

Warehouse Associate (WA)

The warehouse engine of the field operation — stages each day's product against the schedule so crews load and leave fast, and processes the flood of returns that comes back during strike.

TypeSeasonal (heavy), year-round upkeep
Reports toSupply Chain / Warehouse Manager (Tony), via AM/TM
Direct reportsNone in season; directs 2–3 late-season hires + light rollers during strike
Works withST/JT crews, QP, RT, RDC schedule
HubUtah HQ (forklift + cage) or satellite hub (manual, often solo)

Purpose of the role

Get the right product to the right job before the crew shows up — and recover it cleanly when the season comes down.

The Warehouse seat keeps the field supplied. During install season the day is built around crew support: get installers in and out fast in the morning, then spend the afternoon pulling and sectioning the next day's material against the schedule so nothing holds a crew up at 7 AM. A crew waiting on the warehouse is a crew not installing.

During strike the warehouse pivots from outbound logistics to inbound recovery. Material comes back in bulk from takedown crews — the WA breaks down returned jobs, sorts by product and condition, reports actual returned items and damage back into the deal profile, re-rolls and restocks usable inventory, and routes scrap. The WA is the core of the strike warehouse crew, directing light rollers and seasonal hires through the high-volume work.

Procurement and ordering itself sits with Supply Chain (Tony), not the WA — the WA receives, stages, ships, recovers, and keeps the building clean and organized year-round.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Part of the warehouse crew under Supply Chain / Warehouse Manager (Tony), who sets the schedule and checks prep and outbound orders.
  • Day-to-day communication runs through the chain of command: WA → AM → Tony — questions go to Tony directly only when the AM can't answer.
  • At satellite hubs the WA works under TM or AM oversight on site, with Tony available via Slack / FaceTime.
  • Supports adjacent field seats — ST/JT crews loading out, QP and RT during strike, and the RDC's schedule that drives next-day prep.

Handbook note: In the current handbook the next-day schedule the warehouse preps against is finalized by the Territory Manager — a separate seat above the Area Manager — not a dispatch coordinator. This profile reflects the redesigned model where the RDC runs the board and the Territory Manager is an Area Manager stretched over more ground.

Compensation

  • Seasonal-heavy role with year-round warehouse upkeep — Utah WAs start earliest (August) to receive containers; satellite hubs ramp in September.
  • Hourly warehouse pay (not production-share like field crews); the handbook does not publish a rate.
  • Forklift use (Utah) requires certification by Tony as a Phase 2 gate.
  • No dollar figures are defined in the handbook; pay structure is described at a high level only.

Core responsibilities

  • Next-day job prep (daily, install season). Open BriteNow, read the next day's jobs and Lead Installer, pull every product, and section it into a labeled pile per installer. VIP jobs get new product only; everything else uses used product unless noted. Tony checks before end of day.
  • Morning crew support. Get crews loaded and out the door fast — help pick extra material, manage the cage and track tools out (Utah), minimize time at the warehouse.
  • Returns processing (install season, daily). Sort what crews drop off by product type, re-roll strands with proper hand-over-hand technique (never rip a tangle — the #1 damage source), inspect for cuts/missing plugs/exposed copper, box used lights (40 strands max, labeled), and restock accessories.
  • Strike recovery (high volume). Receive returned material in bulk, break down returned jobs, sort by product then by condition (good / damaged / scrap), report actual returned items + damage into the deal profile, and restock usable inventory. No per-job counting on return — the full inventory count happens at end of strike.
  • Outbound shipping. Freight (LTL via FreightCenter — palletize, weigh, measure, BOL) and Southwest Cargo (pull SKUs against Tony's packing list, fill the SWA slip, drive to cargo). Double-check the destination airport — a wrong airport is one of the costliest WA mistakes.
  • Inbound receiving (early season). Unload shipping containers (Utah, August) and LTL freight, sort by product type onto pallets (not by job), shrink-wrap, and shelve. Report damage and discrepancies to Tony.
  • Scrap routing. Keep C9 wire and mini wire separate (C9 prices higher), keep scrap metal out of regular trash, bring ID to the scrap yard, return receipts to Tony.
  • Holiday structures. Transport (26'/20' U-Haul) and store seasonal structures — label components, protect fragile parts, two-person minimum on large items. Assembly and teardown are handled by installers or someone assigned, not the WA.
  • Daily cleanup (non-negotiable, every season). Sweep floors, empty trash, clear trip hazards, return tools, secure the cage (Utah). Leaving the space right every day is a clearance criterion.
  • Errands. Home Depot runs (exact list, return receipts), deliveries, and Noel Decor support — heavy lifting and airport runs — but WAs never pull or pack Noel Decor orders, and never hand off a company gas/credit card.

A day in the role — install season

  1. 7:00–8:00 AM — Crew supportGet installers loaded and out fast. Help pick extra material, manage the cage, track tools going out (Utah). Priority #1: minimize crew time at the warehouse.
  2. Mid-morning — Cleanup & returnsWarehouse is a mess after crews leave. Sort yesterday's returns, re-roll lights, box and restock.
  3. Mid-day — Shipping & errandsPull packing lists, palletize, shrink-wrap, weigh. Southwest Cargo and Home Depot runs, job-site material deliveries, pickups.
  4. Afternoon — Next-day prepTony sends the schedule screenshot via Slack. Pull all material and section it by installer; Tony checks before end of day. Material is staged against the schedule so the next day's jobs are ready to load.
  5. Late afternoon — Downtime & closeNoel Decor support (heavy lifting, airport runs), sweeping, trash, housekeeping. Floor swept, no trip hazards, tools returned, cage secured.

This is the Utah HQ rhythm. Satellite hubs follow the same cadence at lower volume — no forklift, no cage, often one WA solo.

The season arc

PhaseWhenWhat the warehouse is doing
Early / receiving August (Utah) → Sept Unload international containers and LTL freight; sort by product type onto pallets and shelve. Utah WAs hired earliest for this.
Peak / install season Sept → late Dec Daily crew support, next-day prep against the schedule, outbound shipping to territories and hubs, daily returns and cleanup.
Strike season January → ~April Inbound recovery: receive returned jobs in bulk, break them down, sort by product and condition, report returned items + damage into the deal profile, re-roll, restock, scrap. Direct light rollers and 2–3 seasonal hires.
Audit / close-out End of strike → spring Full inventory count, C9 spool cleanup (de-bulb, sort wire for scrap, test bulbs by year), restock and organize, year-round warehouse upkeep.

Who the role works with

Supply Chain / Warehouse Manager — Tony
Sets the daily schedule (Slack screenshot), builds packing lists, checks next-day prep and every outbound order, owns procurement/ordering, tracks inventory counts, and certifies forklift use.
AM (Area Manager) / TM (Territory Manager)
On-site oversight at satellite hubs and the first stop in the chain of command (WA → AM → Tony). The TM or AM may relay the next day's schedule when Tony is remote.
RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator)
Runs the dispatch board that produces the schedule the WA preps against, and owns the end-of-season strike reconciliation the warehouse recovery feeds into.
ST / JT field crews
The customers of the warehouse — they load out in the morning, pick extra material, and drop off returns. Fast load-out keeps their production day intact.
QP (Quality Pro) & RT (Repair Tech)
Both join the warehouse crew during strike, working alongside the WA on takedown recovery, sorting, and processing returns.
LR (Light Rollers)
Seasonal strike crew the WA directs through the repetitive rolling, sorting, and boxing — rotating tasks to prevent burnout and keep the recovery line moving.
