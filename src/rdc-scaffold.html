Purpose of the role

The Regional Dispatch Coordinator runs the board; the Area Manager owns the territory.

The Regional Dispatch Coordinator (RDC) owns the mechanical, real-time coordination of installation work across a cluster of territories — the dispatch board, the first-line customer and operational phone, the scheduling gate with Sales, and the end-of-season strike reconciliation. The Area Manager owns judgment, the customer account, field supervision, and the territory’s financial result.

The RDC is remote and centralized; the Area Manager is locally present and is the direct supervisor of the field crews. By absorbing the dispatch and back-office load, the RDC lets each Area Manager cover more ground than they otherwise could.

The role runs in two distinct modes across its season — Install (throughput) then Strike (reconciliation). The work is persistent across the whole season rather than spiky: as live dispatch grows more demanding during install, reconciliation is light; during strike, dispatch is light while reconciliation becomes the core of the work.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Director of Operations.
  • Peer to the Area Manager — supports them laterally; not above or below, with no direct reports.
  • Complex issues escalate to the Operations Manager, who also partners on larger commercial jobs and provides mobile support to Area Managers.
  • One RDC supports the Area Managers across its region; a Territory Manager is simply an Area Manager carrying a larger or less-developed area.

Handbook note: This is a newly designed seat — the current handbook has no Regional Dispatch Coordinator. Today it assigns the daily schedule, the dispatch board, and crew management to the Territory Manager, and treats the Territory Manager as a separate seat above the Area Manager. This profile reflects the redesigned model: the RDC runs the board, and the Territory Manager is an Area Manager stretched over more ground rather than a tier of its own.

Compensation

  • Seasonal base pay for the ~5.5-month season, set at a level comparable to a standard Area Manager.
  • Eligible for a performance-based end-of-season bonus.
  • The role rewards consistency and reliability — steady, accurate coordination sustained across the full season.

A day in Install · throughput mode

build → launch → staggered streams → keep the board true → close & rebuild
  1. Pre-dispatch (≈6:00–6:30, before crews roll)Review the operations dashboard (weather / call-outs / traffic / staging by area); clear the Area Manager’s overnight queued questions; finalize the last ~20% of the board. The other 80% was built at yesterday’s end-of-day.
  2. Group standup with Area Managers (≈10–15 min)One short, structured call — today’s board, problem jobs, roster changes, capacity to give Sales. Held before crews roll, so it doesn’t collide with the inbound peak.
  3. Launch (≈7:30)Crews roll with a quick “rolling” confirmation from each crew lead. On-site blockers go to the Area Manager.
  4. Repair Tech coordination (≈9:00 · 7:00 in peak)Walk the pre-planned repair route. Same-day where routing allows, otherwise a queue builds — safety or customer escalation overrides and dispatches immediately.
  5. Quality Pro coordination (≈11:00)Job-by-job walk-through with specific notes and handoffs. Target: a quality check within 48 hours of install, once caught up.
  6. Sustained monitoring (midday onward)Keep reality matched to the board — re-slot, feed early-finishing crews, gate Sales’ hard dates, work customer and deferred items, capture incoming QP and repair findings.
  7. End of dayClose every job (photos + notes, supervisor sign-off); reconcile installed-vs-scheduled; roll incompletes forward; build tomorrow’s 80%; post the queue for the Area Manager’s evening review; log crew-day records.

The assignment is also the labor-cost record. Each crew is assigned roughly $4–6k of revenue per day — to a Senior Tech with his Junior Tech(s) attached — which is what makes per-crew, per-job margin measurable.

A day in Strike · reconciliation mode

light sequential dispatch + the consolidation audit

Removal & intake

  • Senior Tech / Junior Tech crews remove — strike is the second half of the same labor cycle. Completing only the install half does not earn the profit share.
  • Crews work tight clusters, sequenced highest-revenue → furthest → back toward the warehouse.
  • Because it’s pre-sequenced, daily board work is light — roll incompletes forward and populate the next jobs. Crews are loaded heavily (~$10–15k, roughly twice the install rate) against the Jan 31 deadline.
  • Returns are labeled by crew · customer · contents (a card per box on multi-box jobs). The warehouse crew reports actual returned items and damage, which updates the job’s deal profile.

The three-way reconciliation

The RDC consolidates signals captured by the people closest to the work.

  • Sales accuracy — line item vs. assigned product: was the job buildable as scoped? (unfinished / impossible quantity).
  • Install accuracy — was the design completed correctly? Primarily the Quality Pro’s in-season flag, corroborated by what comes back.
  • Damage — reported with a brief explanation; whether it was negligence is the Area Manager’s call.
  • Eligibility roll-up — cycle complete + sales flags + quality scores + damage notes are compiled by the RDC; leadership rules on the profit share, with room for discretion.

Who the RDC works with

Area Manager (AM)
The in-field supervisor and owner of the customer account and territory result. The RDC’s primary day-to-day partner. A “Territory Manager” is an Area Manager covering a larger or less-developed area.
Senior Tech (ST)
Crew lead and installer. Earns production pay as a share of installed revenue — contingent on completing the full install-and-strike cycle and meeting install-accuracy standards.
Junior Tech (JT)
Works on a Senior Tech’s crew; attached to the crew for the day, which is how labor cost per job is captured.
Quality Pro (QP)
Inspector role responsible for install accuracy — checking that each design was completed correctly, within 48 hours of install once caught up.
Repair Tech (RT)
Handles repairs and service of failed displays on a planned route, with safety and escalation overrides.
Light Roller (LR)
Seasonal strike support; helps the warehouse crew process returns.
Warehouse (WA)
Stages the next day’s product against the schedule and processes returns during strike.
Sales
Dedicated team that books and renews work and owns the customer through the close, then hands sold jobs to the RDC to schedule.
