Position Profile

Repair Tech (RT)

The solo field specialist who keeps displays lit through the season — diagnosing and rebuilding failed installations one client at a time on a pre-planned route, with same-day overrides when safety or a customer escalation demands it.

TypeSeasonal (Sep–Mar)
Reports to / coordinates withArea Manager (daily)
Direct reportsNone
StaffingTier 1 territories only — 1 per territory
Trained toFull installer competency + diagnostics

Purpose of the role

The swiss army knife of the field — you can't fix what you can't build, so the RT is trained to rebuild every install type, then layers diagnostics, QC, and customer communication on top.

When a display fails after install, Brite Nites doesn't send a full crew for a single callback — it sends the Repair Tech. The RT runs a route of isolated repairs across the territory: a dead roofline section, a slipped clip, a tree strand out, a tripped GFI, a timer that won't fire. Repair is targeted re-installation — the RT diagnoses the failure, then rebuilds the affected section to the same standard as the original install.

The RT is also the most customer-facing solo operator in the field. They arrive alone at a client's home to fix something that's broken — the client is frustrated before the truck pulls up, and the quality of the fix is judged in the moment, by one person. Professional posture and judgment under pressure are core requirements, not nice-to-haves.

Between repairs, the RT runs quality-control checks on completed installs — every RT is QC-trained, which also sharpens diagnostic instinct: you learn what breaks by seeing what's installed poorly.

In an ideal setup the RT runs with a helper. The whole point of the seat is to keep the Senior Techs and Junior Techs installing new sales rather than circling back on callbacks — and when a crew leaves a quality issue behind, they forfeit the profit share on that job, while the labor cost that stays with it helps fund the repair crew. A helper lets the RT work both lanes at once: while the RT runs a quality check, the helper clears the minor repairs it turns up. In a lull, the helper organizes the vehicle or trains on the QC/repair flow — a little idle time isn't an immediate loss, and it's what lets one RT cover both repairs and quality. The helper can be full- or part-time, and the RT may still run solo.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Coordinates daily with the Area Manager (AM). The AM is the RT's daily co-pilot — primary trainer, assigner of appointments, and the 9am review partner who carries client history and territory priorities the RT can't see.
  • Hired and gated by the Area Manager (AM) — runs MEWP certification and owns territory-level repair metrics.
  • Solo or with an assistant. Ideally the RT either runs solo or is paired with a dedicated assistant — full- or part-time — who clears minor repairs while the RT runs quality checks. The assistant is not the Warehouse Associate: warehouse operations stay busy and the WA also covers Noel Decor. A WA might fill in when there's truly no other option, but it isn't the plan.
  • Tier 1 only. A dedicated seat staffed in larger territories; smaller markets handle repairs informally through the AM and STs.

Handbook note: The daily 9am Area Manager review in this profile matches the handbook. The handbook does not, however, cite a national repair average under 18 hours or a set evening-coverage standard — it describes flexible territory repair windows that can shift the start/end by an hour or two. It also treats the Territory Manager as a separate seat above the Area Manager.

Compensation & engagement

  • Seasonal role — hired in early September, released after strike (roughly April–August off). Off-season retention happens only in rare, exceptional circumstances.
  • First-hire is the primary path. Unlike the Senior Tech, the RT does not require prior Brite experience — the right profile (reliable, efficient, comfortable with customers) is trained from scratch to full installer competency.
  • Season-over-season rehire runs through the abbreviated Returning-RT refresher (~1 week) rather than a full re-train.
  • Pay structure follows the seasonal field model; specific figures are set by the territory and not part of this profile.

Core responsibilities

  • Diagnose and repair failed displays — confirm the reported issue, catch adjacent defects, and rebuild the affected section to install standard. Roofline (wind/clip/bulb/water) is the most common category; tree & greenery second; hanging-light, specialty, and electrical faults follow.
  • Run a daily repair route — 4–8 stops across the territory, ordered for efficiency around committed client windows, executed solo.
  • Turn repairs around same-day — same-day service is the standard, not the exception: Brite's national repair average runs under 18 hours. Because displays fail and clients call once it's dark, the RT covers into the evening — often until ~9pm — to clear urgent fixes the day they come in.
  • Communicate professionally at every door — clean arrival, blame-neutral damage language, a clear timeline within the first two minutes, and a visible walkthrough at sign-off. No upselling, no lingering.
  • Document every visit — submit a service report (with photos for major fixes) in BriteNow before leaving the property, within 60 minutes of the stop.
  • Run quality-control checks between repairs on completed installs — flag defects to the AM, fix minor issues on the spot, schedule a return for major ones.
  • Handle additional installs during peak — greenery and lighting add-ons, and crew/commercial support as needed.
  • Cover the service period solo (mid-Dec–early Jan) — the primary point of contact when holiday lights fail; fast turnaround protects the client base.
  • Join the warehouse crew during strike — solo takedowns and processing returned material (sorting restockable / broken / discard) alongside the warehouse team.
  • Escalate by the rule, not by judgment — two-person jobs, structural damage, panel/main-line electrical work, confrontational clients, and over-window repairs all go to the AM. Work beyond strand / plug / GFI-outlet level is always licensed-electrician scope.

A day in the role

A repair day is appointment-driven, not site-driven — many clients, many problems, many fixes. It runs on a five-stage rhythm anchored by the AM touchpoint — and same-day calls reshape it constantly: when a display goes dark in the evening, the RT may run later (toward ~9pm) to clear it that night.

  1. 8:30 AM — Pre-review setupArrive at the warehouse, walk-around the truck, pull the day's ticket queue in BriteNow, and flag anything unusual for the AM.
  2. ~9:00 AM — AM review (15–20 min)The highest-leverage 20 minutes of the day. Priorities, per-appointment client history, parts needed, the weather call, and yesterday's open follow-ups. In peak, the day starts earlier — closer to 7am — to fit more stops.
  3. 9:20 AM — Routing & loadingPlan the most efficient route around committed windows; load the standing kit plus the day's specific parts. Don't depart without the parts identified in review — missing parts mean a second trip.
  4. 10:00 AM onward — Execution loopPer stop: arrive & introduce → communicate & set timeline → diagnose → repair → document in BriteNow before leaving → next. Call the AM from site if any stop runs past 2× its expected time. On same-day-call days the loop runs into the evening rather than ending mid-afternoon.
  5. Late afternoon / evening — Wrap & reportStage the truck for tomorrow, submit any outstanding service reports, flag restock needs, and route schedule-return follow-ups to the AM (the RT does not self-schedule returns). On a quiet day this lands mid-afternoon; on a heavy same-day-call day it's after the last evening fix — coverage runs to ~9pm.

The RT must clear height work — a go/no-go in training. They carry MEWP certification and a full JT-equivalent skills battery, because they make the fix solo, at height, on a client's property.

Who the role works with

Area Manager (AM)
The daily co-pilot. Primary trainer during onboarding, then the permanent 9am-review partner — assigns appointments, carries client history and territory priorities, makes the weather call, routes follow-ups, and is the first escalation for anything beyond the RT's solo scope.
RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator)
The RT coordinates with the RDC on scheduling and dispatch, and routes back-office questions — hours, pay, HR — there. Day-to-day field direction and the 9am review stay with the AM.
Senior Techs (STs)
Field mentors during the supervised phase and the source of crew assists when a repair needs a second set of hands. When there's no RT in a territory, repair volume falls back onto the STs.
Quality Pro (QP)
Where a territory staffs a dedicated QP, they carry the systematic QC load; the RT still runs a handful of checks on the jobs they're already on. In areas that can't support both a Repair Tech and a dedicated QP, the AM shares the QC load. Both share the warehouse crew during strike.
Warehouse Associates (WAs)
The strike-season teammates the RT processes returns alongside. The WA is not the RT's repair helper, though — warehouse operations stay busy and the WA also covers Noel Decor; a WA only fills in on repairs as a last resort.
Clients
The RT is the face of the fix — often the only Brite person a client meets one-on-one. Every repair is a relationship moment that decides whether the client returns next season.
