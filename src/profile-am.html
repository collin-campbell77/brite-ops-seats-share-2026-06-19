Area Manager (AM)

The in-field boss. The Area Manager owns the customer account and the daily operation of a territory — supervising field crews, clearing on-site blockers, running the warehouse, and owning the territory's financial result.

Purpose of the Role

The RDC runs the board; the AM owns the territory.

The Area Manager is the area operations owner — the single person who knows every job in flight, every crew status, and every customer issue in the territory today. The AM is the bridge between the operating decisions made above (scheduling, reporting, people) and the crews' daily work on the ground. The role is hands-on operational management, not administration: AMs spend their time in the warehouse and on job sites, not at a desk.

An AM is built from the most experienced field operators in the area — typically a Senior Technician promoted into the management layer, or an external hire with field-operations management experience. The job is not to be the best installer; it is to understand every aspect of the work well enough to manage it, coach it, inspect it, and fill the gap when something breaks.

The AM is the territory's anchor through the season — and year-round is the goal as territories mature, though it depends on the location and the sales plan and isn't promised to every AM yet. A Territory Manager (TM) is the same seat stretched over a larger or under-developed area — a temporary stopgap that lets one operator hold more ground, not a higher tier. AM and TM are closer to peers than their titles suggest: the AM owns execution on the ground while the RDC runs the scheduling board and backend.

Reporting & Compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Director of Operations; the Operations Manager develops and coaches the Area Manager bench.
  • Directly supervises the Senior Techs (crew leads) and Junior Techs working in the area.
  • Coordinates daily with the Warehouse Associate on next-day prep — though the WA reports up the Warehouse function, not Field Ops.
  • Peer to the RDC — the RDC runs dispatch and the scheduling board across areas; the AM owns the territory on the ground.
  • Backed by the Operations Manager as the cross-territory training-and-quality standard resource (a resource, not a line manager).

Handbook note: The current handbook represents this seat more narrowly — the Area Manager reports to a Territory Manager (a separate seat above the AM that owns the daily schedule, crew prep, and hiring), is scoped to maintenance and install inspection, and is described as the territory's year-round anchor. This profile reflects the redesigned model: the AM as the territory's full in-field owner reporting to the Director of Operations, the Territory Manager as an AM stretched over more ground, and the RDC running the dispatch board.

Compensation & tenure

  • Salaried management seat, not production pay. The AM manages the people who install and strike; they do not earn the technicians' revenue share.
  • Seasonal today; year-round is the goal. Whether an AM runs year-round depends on the location and next year's sales plan — the direction we're building toward, not a promise to every AM yet. First-season AMs typically start around August (location-dependent) and are cleared before peak; once cleared, the AM is the territory's anchor through peak and strike.
  • Returning AMs ramp in ~3–5 days (recertification + area re-familiarization).
  • Growth path: cover a larger territory, then Regional / Commercial Operations. Most common outcome is returning as AM in good standing.

Pay structure shown at a high level only; specific figures live in the territory's offer.

Core Responsibilities

Customer account & territory ownership

  • Owns the customer account in the territory — the in-field point of accountability for the work landing on a client's property.
  • Owns the territory's financial and operational result; if the area runs poorly, that lands on the AM, not the crew or the WA.
  • Makes the damage / negligence judgment on-site — assessing whether a property issue is wear, install error, or negligence, and handling or escalating it. Property issues with legal/insurance exposure escalate to the RDC and Client Success.
  • Sees the area's process and workflow through — warehouse organization, prep standards, and how the day is sequenced inside the territory — running it day to day and putting forward improvements they find valuable. The underlying scaffolding isn't theirs to author outright; they implement and refine it, not own every aspect of it.

Crew supervision & on-site blockers

  • Supervises Senior Techs and Junior Techs in the field; runs the morning brief and load-out.
  • Assists the RDC in monitoring crew progress live via the Crew App — a help-when-there's-downtime duty, since the AM is primarily expected to be in the field, not at a screen. Right-sizes crews (2-person preferred, 3 when scope requires) and reassigns when jobs run over or conditions change. When the RDC asks the AM to look at a job, the AM goes — collaborating on the quality standard and coordinating the next training steps.
  • Is the area's mobile supply line and real-time answer — clears field questions, scope ambiguity, material shortfalls, and tool problems on the spot, not "I'll get back to you."
  • Manages VIP and escalation-prone jobs physically on-site, not from a status text.

Field quality — the inspection gate

  • Runs early-season quality checks and ongoing inspection — the gate before a job ships to billing.
  • Physically verifies the work: gets out of the truck and checks connections, cables, light counts, and timer function. Confirming the lights are on from the curb is not an inspection — the job isn't verified until it's been touched. This is the QP's job once a QP is in place; early season the AM does it directly — both to hold the line and to learn the process well enough to report the quality level and train a QP when that time comes.
  • Gets dispatched to recurring-problem properties to resolve them permanently.

Warehouse operations

  • Owns daily warehouse execution — directs WAs on next-day kits, material staging, and truck loading; inspects the prior night's pulls before crews touch them.
  • Tracks inventory and flags reorder needs to the RDC — procurement itself is owned by the Warehouse Manager / Supply Chain, not the AM.
  • Holds the warehouse to a consistent standard of organization, prep accuracy, and pace.

Coaching & training

  • Coaches STs and JTs in the field and runs team huddles — developing crews, not just supervising them.
  • Delivers compressed mid-season onboarding for new JTs and WAs in the territory; the ability to train others is a clearance criterion.
  • Surfaces patterns up to the RDC — and, where it fits, the Operations Manager — reorders, recurring problem sites, crew issues — instead of absorbing them silently.

A Day in the Role

A long split day, sized to the area — warehouse and office in the morning, field oversight in the afternoon, closeout to set up tomorrow. The AM's hours follow the operational needs of the territory, not a set end time. The structured blocks below are the spine, but the day ends when the area's work is handled, and the AM stays on call after dark for maintenance and customer issues — displays get judged once they're lit, and that's when the calls come in.

  1. ~6:00 AM — Schedule review & edit Confirm nothing changed overnight — cancellations, scope shifts, crew callouts, last-minute adds. Reorder jobs and reassign crews before anyone is loaded.
  2. ~6:15 AM — Inspect WA job pulls Walk the warehouse and verify the night-before prep against today's jobs — counts, labeling, specialty items. Prep failures are recoverable at 6:30, not at the jobsite at 7:30.
  3. ~7:00 AM — Manage installer arrival Run a fast per-crew day review, support the WA-led load-out, answer field questions in real time, and call a short process review when there's a real signal.
  4. 8:00–10:00 AM — Direct WAs + slotted computer tasks Set warehouse priorities for the day after crews depart; slot in RDC coordination, email, and reporting during calm windows. Wrapping this block by 10 leaves a little extra time to keep an eye on crew progress. The morning block is the core of the job.
  5. 11:00 AM–1:00 PM — Lunch (movable window, ~1 hr) A movable window, not a fixed time — eat after the warehouse clears or on the road if a field issue surfaces — but the break itself should run about an hour, not the full two-hour window. A long day doesn't run without fuel.
  6. 1:00–5:00 PM — Field oversight Three to five jobsite touches: crew shadowing, in-context coaching, supplies support, quality checks and small repairs, VIP jobs. This is where the area's quality is built or lost.
  7. ~5:00–8:00 PM — End-of-day closeout Receive crews as they come off later jobs, debrief, handle personnel flags, coordinate with the RDC, and lock tomorrow's schedule and prep. With longer field days the wind-down runs into the evening. Closeout is the bridge between today and tomorrow.
  8. Evening — on call The structured day ends, but the AM stays reachable after dark for maintenance and customer issues — a display that won't light, an escalation, an urgent next-day fix. Not a second shift every night; the coverage that comes with owning the territory.

A note on hours, for discussion: the workload isn't even across the season. Early on, there's a good chance the area doesn't need people on the clock as long, and keeping hours contained there may help people stay fresh. During peak that flips — you put in the extra-long hours to get done what has to get done. The idea is simply to match hours to the work actually in front of the area rather than run long all season.

Who the Role Works With

RDC — Regional Dispatch Coordinator
The AM's peer. The RDC runs the dispatch board across several areas and is the first-line customer/operational phone. The handoff line: the RDC runs the board; the AM owns the territory.
Senior Techs (ST) & Junior Techs (JT)
The crews the AM supervises, dispatches, inspects, and coaches. STs lead crews and execute installs; the AM briefs them each morning and verifies their work before it ships.
Warehouse Associate (WA)
Stages next-day kits and processes returns. The AM directs the warehouse work daily and inspects pulls, though the WA reports up the Warehouse function rather than Field Ops.
Quality Pro (QP)
The inspector model for install accuracy. QP findings of systematic issues are a signal the AM's own field-inspection gate may need tightening.
Repair Tech (RT)
Once both are cleared, the AM runs a daily co-pilot review with the Tier-1 RT — not training, just how the role works day to day.
Warehouse Manager / Supply Chain
Owns procurement. The AM communicates reorder needs upward (via the RDC) and holds the warehouse to the operational standard set in Utah; the AM does not run procurement.
Client Success & Director of Operations
Client Success owns customer retention/renewal; property issues with legal or insurance exposure escalate there and to the RDC. The Director of Operations sets cross-territory strategy and signs off on the AM's path to a larger territory or the next operations seat.
