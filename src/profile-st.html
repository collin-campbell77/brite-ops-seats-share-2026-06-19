Position Profile

Senior Tech (ST)

The crew lead and installer who owns a job from arrival to client sign-off — directing a crew of Junior Techs through the install, then the strike, and getting paid on the revenue the crew puts up.

TypeSeasonal field role
Reports toArea Manager (AM)
Direct reports1–3 Junior Techs (JT)
PayProduction-based (share of installed revenue)
Sits inField crew (territory-based)

Purpose of the role

An ST stops being measured on the work they do, and starts being measured on the work the crew does.

The Senior Tech is the senior field technician who leads a small crew — the person on site who turns a sold design into a finished, Brite-quality display. The ST directs the Junior Tech(s), makes the wiring and routing calls, enforces safety as the competent person on site, engages the customer at the door, and documents the completed job.

The ST owns the full labor cycle for the crew: the install in Peak season and the strike (removal) in Strike season are two halves of the same job. The crew is handed a daily revenue load by the RDC, and the ST runs the day to clear it accurately and safely.

It is widely treated as one of the most important seats in operations — the multiplier point where individual technique becomes crew output, and where install quality is either built in or lost.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Area Manager (AM), who trains the ST, observes crew leading, and makes promotion/gate decisions.
  • Leads a crew of 1–3 Junior Techs (JT), who execute tasks under the ST's direction.
  • The crew's daily revenue load is assigned by the RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator), who runs the dispatch board.
  • A "Territory Manager" is the same Area Manager seat stretched over a larger area — the AM owns the territory result either way.
  • Work is verified downstream by the Quality Pro (QP) install-accuracy check and the end-of-season strike audit.

Handbook note: The current handbook titles this seat Senior Field Technician (Crew Leader); it reports to the Territory Manager and coordinates day-to-day with the Area Manager, and the daily schedule and crew loads come from the Territory Manager rather than a dispatch coordinator. This profile uses the redesigned naming and model. (The Crew Leader's accountability for all job data does match the handbook.)

Compensation

  • Production pay — the ST earns a share of the revenue the crew installs.
  • Pay is contingent on completing the full install and strike cycle for the crew's work — the strike is not optional or separate.
  • It is also contingent on meeting install-accuracy standards, confirmed by QP checks and the strike audit. Work that fails inspection doesn't count as cleanly produced.
  • Seasonal: paid through Peak/Install season, the in-season service tail, and Strike season.

Pay structure shown at a high level only; specific rates are set per territory.

Core responsibilities

  • Run the job end to end — lead the morning walkthrough, set the crew's plan, and own the job from arrival to sign-off without needing AM direction.
  • Direct the crew — assign each JT's work, keep them moving, and correct technique respectfully per the management protocol.
  • Make wiring decisions — choose the most discrete location and the most efficient route for running power.
  • Enforce safety — as the competent person on site, hold ladder, lift, and electrical standards under production pressure, not just in training.
  • Own the customer interaction — handle the door knock, scope confirmation, change orders, and on-site escalations. Never promise an unconfirmed return date — escalate to the TM/AM instead.
  • Control product use — place only the number of strands specified in the app to avoid over-installation; confirm with the crew leader before adding more and track the total installed.
  • Own the job data in the app — the ST inputs all job data — photos, video, strand and product counts, and completion — and is accountable for it being correct. What the ST enters is what the office sees: it's the record job costing, the yearly product order, and pay all run on, so getting it right is the ST's responsibility, not something the office has to chase down.
  • Lead the strike — remove the crew's installed displays in Strike season and feed clean returns back to the warehouse.
  • Train the next crew — mentor JTs during their final crew phase; this is the ST's core multiplier function.

A day in the role

  1. Clock in at the warehouseArrive on time and clock in on the Deputy app. Lateness is treated as a real problem — it shifts the whole crew's day.
  2. Senior Tech morning coordinationA 15–30 minute sync with the AM: walk every job on the day's schedule, the special trainings to reinforce, and any special items on specific jobs. The AM also coordinates their own day with the ST here — flagging which of the crew's jobs they plan to shadow.
  3. Sign off on the truckThe JTs load the truck — clean cab and bed, prior-day trash and unused product cleared, the day's material loaded against the schedule. The ST's job is the final check that it's prepped right. Roll out of the warehouse for the first job no later than ~9 AM.
  4. Drive to the first job & walk itLead the crew walkthrough on arrival, set the plan, and brief each JT on their piece.
  5. Install across the day's jobsThe crew runs multiple jobs a day, not one — direct them through rooflines, trees, stakes, strands, spheres, wreaths, bows, garland, and timers, making routing calls and metering strand counts to avoid over-installation. The ST is expected to clear every job assigned for the day and hit the company's daily revenue minimum across them.
  6. Document & close out each jobAt every job: capture photos, video, and counts; confirm scope with the customer; record completion in the job-management app; and leave the site clean — police for trash and tools and tie down ladder slack before driving to the next. The ST owns the accuracy of everything entered — the office works off this record, not a second source.
  7. End of day — stage, offload, clock outAfter the last job, return to the warehouse, stage leftover product, and offload the vehicle. Then geo-tracked clock-out and close the loop with the AM.

No two days look alike — rooflines one day, a full day in a tree the next, a mix of every product the day after — but the leadership wrapper around the day stays constant. In Strike season the same daily rhythm runs in reverse: the crew removes displays and returns product instead of installing it.

Who the role works with

Area Manager (AM)
The ST's in-field boss — trains the ST, observes crew leading, owns the territory result, makes the damage/negligence call, and takes escalations the ST can't resolve on site.
Junior Tech (JT)
The ST's crew. JTs execute assigned tasks under the ST's direction and are attached to the crew for the day. The ST also mentors JTs in their final crew phase.
RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator)
Runs the dispatch board and hands the crew its daily revenue load and schedule; the first-line operational and customer phone.
Quality Pro (QP)
Inspects the crew's installs for accuracy against the design — the check that production pay depends on.
Warehouse (WA)
Stages the next day's product against the schedule that the crew loads each morning, and processes the returns the crew brings in during strike.
Territory Manager (TM)
Not a separate seat — the Area Manager stretched across a larger area. In that mode it hires STs and is the destination for return-date and scheduling escalations.
