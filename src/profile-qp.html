Purpose of the role

The QP is an inspector, not an installer — it grades work that's already complete and turns "looks done" into measurable accountability.

Brite Nites installs thousands of designs a season, and the QP exists to verify each one was built correctly. The QP arrives at a completed job, walks it against the design in the app, fixes small things on the spot, and assigns the crew a 1–5 quality score. Those scores are the company's quality signal — they feed crew accountability and the end-of-season strike audit, and they bear on each Senior Tech's eligibility for production pay.

The QP carries no install stations of its own. Its targets are speed and honesty: a quality check ideally lands within 48 hours of install once the territory is caught up. Early in the season, before a QP is ramped, the AM performs the quality checks; once the QP is up to speed, that work shifts over.

The QP is also the "face of Brite Nites" at the home — well-groomed, professional, and trusted by the client. It is the seat that asks for the 5-star review and leaves the property cleaner than the crew found it.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Area Manager (AM) — the QP's day-to-day manager, who coordinates its schedule and warehouse-support needs and (early season) covers quality checks until the QP ramps.
  • Coordinates daily with the RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator), starting around 11am, to confirm the day's route and the next-day punch list.
  • Inspection standards are owned by the Area Manager — the territory needs one consistent quality bar; the QP applies it, it doesn't set it.
  • No direct reports. The QP scores the ST + JT crews but does not manage them.

Handbook note: Two differences from the current handbook: placing the yard sign is not currently a handbook QP duty, and the handbook runs the QP's ~11am coordination with the Territory Manager / Area Manager rather than a dispatch coordinator (the TM being a separate seat above the AM). This profile reflects the redesigned model and the added signage duty.

Compensation

  • Seasonal role — staffed for Peak/Install season and Strike season, released off-season.
  • $50 per verified 5-star review the QP earns from a client (proof required — name in the review or a screenshot), paid in December.
  • Pay structure beyond the review bonus is set per territory; the QP is not on installer production pay (it inspects rather than installs).

Figures shown are the only dollar amounts the handbook states for this seat.

Core responsibilities

  • Verify install accuracy. Walk every package in the app against what was built — full installs, Phase 1 (installed, not yet on), Phase 2 (turn-on / greenery), and strike jobs. Confirm the design matches, lights work lit and unlit, and timers are set to the correct AM/PM windows.
  • Score and rank the crew. End every visit with an honest, consistent 1–5 quality rating in BriteNow, with notes and photos. Scores of 2 or 1 require action and route a return.
  • Fix the small stuff on-site. Handle quick maintenances solo — bulbs, bad strands, timer resets, splicing a socket, GFI swaps, standing up run-over stake lights, hiding cords, plugging in the job.
  • Leave it cleaner than found. Clear trash, broken branches, and debris; protect plants and grass; tuck wires; wipe fingerprints and mud. "Leave the home better than when we arrived."
  • Keep the app honest. Confirm the crew entered correct QTY installed — accurate job costing depends on it and it drives the yearly product order. Take photos, enter notes, document issues before fixing them.
  • Own the client relationship. Be the trusted, confiding face at the door; listen more than talk; earn the 5-star review. Refer to them as clients, not customers. Because the client has met the QP in person, the QP is also a natural point of contact for them to raise needs — a repair, a question, a follow-up — which the QP captures and routes into the system (a repair ticket, or to the AM/RDC). Many clients would rather reach someone they've met in the field than call a general line.
  • Place the yard sign. Set the Brite Nites yard sign at the property and confirm it's upright, visible, and reads from the street — the neighborhood-facing advertisement that turns a finished install into the next lead.
  • Triage, don't repair. Anything needing real rework opens a repair ticket; field questions route to AM/RDC. The QP documents, escalates, and moves on to protect the day's punch-list throughput.
  • Run product when needed. Drive product to crews or add finishing touches (a wreath bow, a small red tree) so jobs land on time.

The 1–5 quality scale

ScoreLabelWhat it meansReturn?
5PerfectMatches the work order exactly. Clean, no damage, all lights working, wiring hidden. Nothing to fix.No
4Minor issuesSmall cleanup or cosmetic fix handled on-site in under 5 minutes.No
3Messy but completeInstall is correct, but cleanup took real effort — scattered trash, sloppy wiring sorted out.No
2Needs a returnSignificant issues the QP can't resolve on-site — missing packages, parts needed, or verifiable damage.Yes
1Serious failureCareless work — wires/clips everywhere, property damage, safety issues. Crew gets direct ST/AM feedback.Yes

Photos are the proof. A score with photos is data; a score without them is an opinion. Honest, consistent scoring across QPs is what makes the whole accountability loop work.

A day in the role

  1. ~11am — sync with the RDCConfirm today's route and review the next-day punch list. Stock the vehicle with spares (bulbs, strands, stakes, timers, wire) for on-site fixes.
  2. Pull up the routeThe office routes the day by urgency, access, and efficiency. Follow the job order exactly and refresh the iPad often — maintenances and reroutes get added through the day.
  3. Arrive professionallyThe office has already told the client the QP is coming, with a photo and a description. Check app notes before getting out of the vehicle.
  4. Inspect against the designWalk every package in the app. Confirm the install matches, lights work lit and unlit, timers are correct, QTY is entered right. Photograph any issue before touching it.
  5. Fix what's quickSwap bulbs, replace a bad strand, reset a timer, splice a socket, hide a cord. If it can't be fixed on-site, document it and route a return.
  6. Clean & close the propertyTrash, branches, debris, fingerprints, trampled plants, tire marks — leave it better than the crew did. Run the before-you-leave checklist.
  7. Plant the yard signSet the Brite Nites yard sign where it reads from the street — upright and visible. It's the neighborhood-facing ad that turns a finished install into the next lead.
  8. Score the crew & file the reportEnter the 1–5 rating in BriteNow with notes + photos. Scores of 2 or 1 trigger a return and crew feedback.
  9. Earn the reviewTalk with the client, note one genuine detail about them, and ask for the 5-star review ($50 per verified review).

Who the role works with

Area Manager (AM)
Day-to-day manager. Coordinates the QP's schedule and warehouse-support needs, performs the quality checks early-season until the QP ramps, and acts on the QP's scores with the crews it manages.
RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator)
Daily coordination point (~11am). Runs the dispatch board, confirms the route, and absorbs the next-day punch list the QP's findings generate.
Senior Tech (ST) & Junior Tech (JT) crews
The crews the QP inspects and scores. Their quality averages over the season bear on accountability and on each ST's eligibility for production pay.
Territory Manager (TM)
Not a separate seat — the Area Manager covering a larger area. In that mode the AM sets and owns the inspection standard so the territory grades to one consistent bar.
Repair Tech (RT)
Receives the rework the QP triages — anything beyond a quick on-site fix becomes a repair ticket rather than QP work.
Warehouse (WA) crew
The QP's strike-season team — processing returns together once the season shifts from inspection to takedown.
Clients
The QP is the trusted, professional face at the door — the relationship that drives retention and earns the 5-star review, and often the person a client reaches out to directly with a need like a repair (which the QP routes into a ticket) rather than calling a general line.
