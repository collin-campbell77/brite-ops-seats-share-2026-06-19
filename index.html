Position Profile

Director of Operations

The top of the operations org — accountable for the core operation company-wide, the season plan, and the structure of seats beneath it.

TypeYear-round · leadership
ScopeCompany-wide operations
OverseesRDC · AM · field seats
OwnsOperational data & planning

Purpose of the role

Owns the core operation — the seasons run, the structure holds, the numbers are real.

The Director of Operations is accountable for running the company’s core operation across every market: the season plan, the operational structure, and the performance of the seats that execute it. The Regional Dispatch Coordinators and Area Managers ultimately roll up here, and this seat is the holder of the company’s operational data — headcount, market details, season calendars, and crew structure.

It is the steady hand over the whole operational machine: setting the plan each season, staffing and structuring the field, and making the final calls — including pay and profit-share rulings — alongside the Operations Manager.

Reporting & mandate

Where it sits

  • Top of the operations org.
  • The Operations Manager and Regional Dispatch Coordinators report into this seat.
  • The Area Managers and field seats roll up through the operation it runs.

Handbook note: In the current handbook this seat directs the Territory Managers, the Operations Manager, and the Warehouse Manager, with the Area Managers reporting up through the Territory Managers — and there is no Regional Dispatch Coordinator. This profile reflects the redesigned model in which the RDC reports here and the territories roll up without a separate Territory Manager tier.

What it’s accountable for

  • The core operation’s performance, company-wide.
  • The season plan and operational structure.
  • Final authority on pay and profit-share rulings (with the Operations Manager).

Core responsibilities

  • Season planning — sets the operational plan and calendar each season across markets.
  • Operational structure — owns headcount, market structure, and crew structure; decides how the field is organized.
  • Performance oversight — receives the automated operations reporting (installed-vs-scheduled, revenue per crew, quality scores, repair queue, cancels/postponements) and runs the weekly operating rhythm with the RDCs.
  • Final authority — rules on escalations, pay, and profit-share eligibility, applying discretion beyond the raw audit.
  • Operational data steward — the source of truth for headcount, market, and season data the rest of the org plans against.

Who the role works with

Regional Dispatch Coordinators (RDC)
Report into this seat; supply the automated reporting and the weekly operating pulse.

The field-management seats whose territories make up the operation this role runs.
Sales & Supply Chain
Partner functions for pipeline and procurement that the operation depends on.

Operations Manager

The bridge between the company’s growth ambitions and the work that gets done in the field.

Purpose of the role

Builds the capabilities the operation runs on — trained people, a deeper field-management bench, and commercial delivery.

The Operations Manager runs operations across the business — embedded day-to-day with every Area Manager, owning the training function, and developing the Area Manager bench so the field can scale. The scope spans both Brite Nites and Brite Labs, residential and commercial work alike. Where the Director of Operations runs the core operation, this seat builds the next layer of capability beneath it — the people and programs that staff the field, and the delivery of the company’s commercial jobs, where this seat carries particular weight.

Today the seat also directs operations for the AZ market and provides mobile support to Area Managers across regions — stepping in on the road wherever a market or seat needs reinforcement.

The title reflects today’s broad mandate. As the Area Manager bench is built out and the commercial footprint grows nationally, the seat is expected to concentrate on commercial operations — with the title specializing toward Commercial Operations as the business does.

Reporting & mandate

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Director of Operations.
  • Works alongside the Regional Dispatch Coordinators and is a senior resource for Area Managers — escalation, coaching, and the partner on larger commercial jobs.

  • Today: works across all Area Managers + training + commercial jobs + AZ operations + mobile AM support — spanning Brite Nites and Brite Labs.
  • End-state: national commercial operations leader + owner of training.
  • The title is set to today’s broad scope and is expected to specialize toward Commercial Operations (and a Director/VP of Commercial Operations) as the bench is built and the commercial footprint goes national.

  • Commercial operations — owns delivery of commercial jobs: scoping, coordination, and partnering directly with Area Managers on the larger, more complex installs.
  • Training — builds and directs the training program across every operational seat: curriculum, role tracks, and the standards crews are held to.
  • Bench development — grows the Area Manager pipeline so territories can be staffed with developed AMs rather than over-stretched stopgaps.
  • AZ market operations — directs operations for the Arizona market.
  • Mobile field support — provides on-the-road reinforcement to Area Managers across regions where a market or seat needs help.
  • Commercial growth — builds the operational foundation to run commercial work at national scale as the footprint expands.

Regional Dispatch Coordinator

A seasonal, remote “air-traffic-control” role that runs the dispatch board across a cluster of territories — the connective tissue between the office, Sales, and the field crews.

TypeSeasonal · remote
Seasonmid-Sept → end Feb
Span3–5 areas · ≤ ~$7M
Reports toDirector of Operations
Direct reportsNone
Peer toArea Manager

Purpose of the role

The Regional Dispatch Coordinator runs the board; the Area Manager owns the territory.

The Regional Dispatch Coordinator (RDC) owns the mechanical, real-time coordination of installation work across a cluster of territories — the dispatch board, the first-line customer and operational phone, the scheduling gate with Sales, and the end-of-season strike reconciliation. The Area Manager owns judgment, the customer account, field supervision, and the territory’s financial result.

The RDC is remote and centralized; the Area Manager is locally present and is the direct supervisor of the field crews. By absorbing the dispatch and back-office load, the RDC lets each Area Manager cover more ground than they otherwise could.

The role runs in two distinct modes across its season — Install (throughput) then Strike (reconciliation). The work is persistent across the whole season rather than spiky: as live dispatch grows more demanding during install, reconciliation is light; during strike, dispatch is light while reconciliation becomes the core of the work.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Director of Operations.
  • Peer to the Area Manager — supports them laterally; not above or below, with no direct reports.
  • Complex issues escalate to the Operations Manager, who also partners on larger commercial jobs and provides mobile support to Area Managers.
  • One RDC supports the Area Managers across its region; a Territory Manager is simply an Area Manager carrying a larger or less-developed area.

Handbook note: This is a newly designed seat — the current handbook has no Regional Dispatch Coordinator. Today it assigns the daily schedule, the dispatch board, and crew management to the Territory Manager, and treats the Territory Manager as a separate seat above the Area Manager. This profile reflects the redesigned model: the RDC runs the board, and the Territory Manager is an Area Manager stretched over more ground rather than a tier of its own.

Compensation

  • Seasonal base pay for the ~5.5-month season, set at a level comparable to a standard Area Manager.
  • Eligible for a performance-based end-of-season bonus.
  • The role rewards consistency and reliability — steady, accurate coordination sustained across the full season.

A day in Install · throughput mode

build → launch → staggered streams → keep the board true → close & rebuild
  1. Pre-dispatch (≈6:00–6:30, before crews roll)Review the operations dashboard (weather / call-outs / traffic / staging by area); clear the Area Manager’s overnight queued questions; finalize the last ~20% of the board. The other 80% was built at yesterday’s end-of-day.
  2. Group standup with Area Managers (≈10–15 min)One short, structured call — today’s board, problem jobs, roster changes, capacity to give Sales. Held before crews roll, so it doesn’t collide with the inbound peak.
  3. Launch (≈7:30)Crews roll with a quick “rolling” confirmation from each crew lead. On-site blockers go to the Area Manager.
  4. Repair Tech coordination (≈9:00 · 7:00 in peak)Walk the pre-planned repair route. Same-day where routing allows, otherwise a queue builds — safety or customer escalation overrides and dispatches immediately.
  5. Quality Pro coordination (≈11:00)Job-by-job walk-through with specific notes and handoffs. Target: a quality check within 48 hours of install, once caught up.
  6. Sustained monitoring (midday onward)Keep reality matched to the board — re-slot, feed early-finishing crews, gate Sales’ hard dates, work customer and deferred items, capture incoming QP and repair findings.
  7. End of dayClose every job (photos + notes, supervisor sign-off); reconcile installed-vs-scheduled; roll incompletes forward; build tomorrow’s 80%; post the queue for the Area Manager’s evening review; log crew-day records.

The assignment is also the labor-cost record. Each crew is assigned roughly $4–6k of revenue per day — to a Senior Tech with his Junior Tech(s) attached — which is what makes per-crew, per-job margin measurable.

A day in Strike · reconciliation mode

light sequential dispatch + the consolidation audit

Removal & intake

  • Senior Tech / Junior Tech crews remove — strike is the second half of the same labor cycle. Completing only the install half does not earn the profit share.
  • Crews work tight clusters, sequenced highest-revenue → furthest → back toward the warehouse.
  • Because it’s pre-sequenced, daily board work is light — roll incompletes forward and populate the next jobs. Crews are loaded heavily (~$10–15k, roughly twice the install rate) against the Jan 31 deadline.
  • Returns are labeled by crew · customer · contents (a card per box on multi-box jobs). The warehouse crew reports actual returned items and damage, which updates the job’s deal profile.

The three-way reconciliation

The RDC consolidates signals captured by the people closest to the work.

  • Sales accuracy — line item vs. assigned product: was the job buildable as scoped? (unfinished / impossible quantity).
  • Install accuracy — was the design completed correctly? Primarily the Quality Pro’s in-season flag, corroborated by what comes back.
  • Damage — reported with a brief explanation; whether it was negligence is the Area Manager’s call.
  • Eligibility roll-up — cycle complete + sales flags + quality scores + damage notes are compiled by the RDC; leadership rules on the profit share, with room for discretion.

Position Profile

Area Manager (AM)

The in-field boss. The Area Manager owns the customer account and the daily operation of a territory — supervising field crews, clearing on-site blockers, running the warehouse, and owning the territory's financial result.

TypeSeasonal today · year-round is the goal
Reports toDirector of Operations
Direct reportsSenior Techs & Junior Techs in the area
Works closely withRDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator)
EntityBrite Nites · Field Operations
HoursSet by the area's needs · evening on-call

Purpose of the Role

The RDC runs the board; the AM owns the territory.

The Area Manager is the area operations owner — the single person who knows every job in flight, every crew status, and every customer issue in the territory today. The AM is the bridge between the operating decisions made above (scheduling, reporting, people) and the crews' daily work on the ground. The role is hands-on operational management, not administration: AMs spend their time in the warehouse and on job sites, not at a desk.

An AM is built from the most experienced field operators in the area — typically a Senior Technician promoted into the management layer, or an external hire with field-operations management experience. The job is not to be the best installer; it is to understand every aspect of the work well enough to manage it, coach it, inspect it, and fill the gap when something breaks.

The AM is the territory's anchor through the season — and year-round is the goal as territories mature, though it depends on the location and the sales plan and isn't promised to every AM yet. A Territory Manager (TM) is the same seat stretched over a larger or under-developed area — a temporary stopgap that lets one operator hold more ground, not a higher tier. AM and TM are closer to peers than their titles suggest: the AM owns execution on the ground while the RDC runs the scheduling board and backend.

Reporting & Compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Director of Operations; the Operations Manager develops and coaches the Area Manager bench.
  • Directly supervises the Senior Techs (crew leads) and Junior Techs working in the area.
  • Coordinates daily with the Warehouse Associate on next-day prep — though the WA reports up the Warehouse function, not Field Ops.
  • Peer to the RDC — the RDC runs dispatch and the scheduling board across areas; the AM owns the territory on the ground.
  • Backed by the Operations Manager as the cross-territory training-and-quality standard resource (a resource, not a line manager).

Handbook note: The current handbook represents this seat more narrowly — the Area Manager reports to a Territory Manager (a separate seat above the AM that owns the daily schedule, crew prep, and hiring), is scoped to maintenance and install inspection, and is described as the territory's year-round anchor. This profile reflects the redesigned model: the AM as the territory's full in-field owner reporting to the Director of Operations, the Territory Manager as an AM stretched over more ground, and the RDC running the dispatch board.

Compensation & tenure

  • Salaried management seat, not production pay. The AM manages the people who install and strike; they do not earn the technicians' revenue share.
  • Seasonal today; year-round is the goal. Whether an AM runs year-round depends on the location and next year's sales plan — the direction we're building toward, not a promise to every AM yet. First-season AMs typically start around August (location-dependent) and are cleared before peak; once cleared, the AM is the territory's anchor through peak and strike.
  • Returning AMs ramp in ~3–5 days (recertification + area re-familiarization).
  • Growth path: cover a larger territory, then Regional / Commercial Operations. Most common outcome is returning as AM in good standing.

Pay structure shown at a high level only; specific figures live in the territory's offer.

Core Responsibilities

Customer account & territory ownership

  • Owns the customer account in the territory — the in-field point of accountability for the work landing on a client's property.
  • Owns the territory's financial and operational result; if the area runs poorly, that lands on the AM, not the crew or the WA.
  • Makes the damage / negligence judgment on-site — assessing whether a property issue is wear, install error, or negligence, and handling or escalating it. Property issues with legal/insurance exposure escalate to the RDC and Client Success.
  • Sees the area's process and workflow through — warehouse organization, prep standards, and how the day is sequenced inside the territory — running it day to day and putting forward improvements they find valuable. The underlying scaffolding isn't theirs to author outright; they implement and refine it, not own every aspect of it.

Crew supervision & on-site blockers

  • Supervises Senior Techs and Junior Techs in the field; runs the morning brief and load-out.
  • Assists the RDC in monitoring crew progress live via the Crew App — a help-when-there's-downtime duty, since the AM is primarily expected to be in the field, not at a screen. Right-sizes crews (2-person preferred, 3 when scope requires) and reassigns when jobs run over or conditions change. When the RDC asks the AM to look at a job, the AM goes — collaborating on the quality standard and coordinating the next training steps.
  • Is the area's mobile supply line and real-time answer — clears field questions, scope ambiguity, material shortfalls, and tool problems on the spot, not "I'll get back to you."
  • Manages VIP and escalation-prone jobs physically on-site, not from a status text.

Field quality — the inspection gate

  • Runs early-season quality checks and ongoing inspection — the gate before a job ships to billing.
  • Physically verifies the work: gets out of the truck and checks connections, cables, light counts, and timer function. Confirming the lights are on from the curb is not an inspection — the job isn't verified until it's been touched. This is the QP's job once a QP is in place; early season the AM does it directly — both to hold the line and to learn the process well enough to report the quality level and train a QP when that time comes.
  • Gets dispatched to recurring-problem properties to resolve them permanently.

Warehouse operations

  • Owns daily warehouse execution — directs WAs on next-day kits, material staging, and truck loading; inspects the prior night's pulls before crews touch them.
  • Tracks inventory and flags reorder needs to the RDC — procurement itself is owned by the Warehouse Manager / Supply Chain, not the AM.
  • Holds the warehouse to a consistent standard of organization, prep accuracy, and pace.

Coaching & training

  • Coaches STs and JTs in the field and runs team huddles — developing crews, not just supervising them.
  • Delivers compressed mid-season onboarding for new JTs and WAs in the territory; the ability to train others is a clearance criterion.
  • Surfaces patterns up to the RDC — and, where it fits, the Operations Manager — reorders, recurring problem sites, crew issues — instead of absorbing them silently.

A Day in the Role

A long split day, sized to the area — warehouse and office in the morning, field oversight in the afternoon, closeout to set up tomorrow. The AM's hours follow the operational needs of the territory, not a set end time. The structured blocks below are the spine, but the day ends when the area's work is handled, and the AM stays on call after dark for maintenance and customer issues — displays get judged once they're lit, and that's when the calls come in.

  1. ~6:00 AM — Schedule review & edit Confirm nothing changed overnight — cancellations, scope shifts, crew callouts, last-minute adds. Reorder jobs and reassign crews before anyone is loaded.
  2. ~6:15 AM — Inspect WA job pulls Walk the warehouse and verify the night-before prep against today's jobs — counts, labeling, specialty items. Prep failures are recoverable at 6:30, not at the jobsite at 7:30.
  3. ~7:00 AM — Manage installer arrival Run a fast per-crew day review, support the WA-led load-out, answer field questions in real time, and call a short process review when there's a real signal.
  4. 8:00–10:00 AM — Direct WAs + slotted computer tasks Set warehouse priorities for the day after crews depart; slot in RDC coordination, email, and reporting during calm windows. Wrapping this block by 10 leaves a little extra time to keep an eye on crew progress. The morning block is the core of the job.
  5. 11:00 AM–1:00 PM — Lunch (movable window, ~1 hr) A movable window, not a fixed time — eat after the warehouse clears or on the road if a field issue surfaces — but the break itself should run about an hour, not the full two-hour window. A long day doesn't run without fuel.
  6. 1:00–5:00 PM — Field oversight Three to five jobsite touches: crew shadowing, in-context coaching, supplies support, quality checks and small repairs, VIP jobs. This is where the area's quality is built or lost.
  7. ~5:00–8:00 PM — End-of-day closeout Receive crews as they come off later jobs, debrief, handle personnel flags, coordinate with the RDC, and lock tomorrow's schedule and prep. With longer field days the wind-down runs into the evening. Closeout is the bridge between today and tomorrow.
  8. Evening — on call The structured day ends, but the AM stays reachable after dark for maintenance and customer issues — a display that won't light, an escalation, an urgent next-day fix. Not a second shift every night; the coverage that comes with owning the territory.

A note on hours, for discussion: the workload isn't even across the season. Early on, there's a good chance the area doesn't need people on the clock as long, and keeping hours contained there may help people stay fresh. During peak that flips — you put in the extra-long hours to get done what has to get done. The idea is simply to match hours to the work actually in front of the area rather than run long all season.

Position Profile

Senior Tech (ST)

The crew lead and installer who owns a job from arrival to client sign-off — directing a crew of Junior Techs through the install, then the strike, and getting paid on the revenue the crew puts up.

TypeSeasonal field role
Reports toArea Manager (AM)
Direct reports1–3 Junior Techs (JT)
PayProduction-based (share of installed revenue)
Sits inField crew (territory-based)

Purpose of the role

An ST stops being measured on the work they do, and starts being measured on the work the crew does.

The Senior Tech is the senior field technician who leads a small crew — the person on site who turns a sold design into a finished, Brite-quality display. The ST directs the Junior Tech(s), makes the wiring and routing calls, enforces safety as the competent person on site, engages the customer at the door, and documents the completed job.

The ST owns the full labor cycle for the crew: the install in Peak season and the strike (removal) in Strike season are two halves of the same job. The crew is handed a daily revenue load by the RDC, and the ST runs the day to clear it accurately and safely.

It is widely treated as one of the most important seats in operations — the multiplier point where individual technique becomes crew output, and where install quality is either built in or lost.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Reports to the Area Manager (AM), who trains the ST, observes crew leading, and makes promotion/gate decisions.
  • Leads a crew of 1–3 Junior Techs (JT), who execute tasks under the ST's direction.
  • The crew's daily revenue load is assigned by the RDC (Regional Dispatch Coordinator), who runs the dispatch board.
  • A "Territory Manager" is the same Area Manager seat stretched over a larger area — the AM owns the territory result either way.
  • Work is verified downstream by the Quality Pro (QP) install-accuracy check and the end-of-season strike audit.

Handbook note: The current handbook titles this seat Senior Field Technician (Crew Leader); it reports to the Territory Manager and coordinates day-to-day with the Area Manager, and the daily schedule and crew loads come from the Territory Manager rather than a dispatch coordinator. This profile uses the redesigned naming and model. (The Crew Leader's accountability for all job data does match the handbook.)

Compensation

  • Production pay — the ST earns a share of the revenue the crew installs.
  • Pay is contingent on completing the full install and strike cycle for the crew's work — the strike is not optional or separate.
  • It is also contingent on meeting install-accuracy standards, confirmed by QP checks and the strike audit. Work that fails inspection doesn't count as cleanly produced.
  • Seasonal: paid through Peak/Install season, the in-season service tail, and Strike season.

Pay structure shown at a high level only; specific rates are set per territory.

Core responsibilities

  • Run the job end to end — lead the morning walkthrough, set the crew's plan, and own the job from arrival to sign-off without needing AM direction.
  • Direct the crew — assign each JT's work, keep them moving, and correct technique respectfully per the management protocol.
  • Make wiring decisions — choose the most discrete location and the most efficient route for running power.
  • Enforce safety — as the competent person on site, hold ladder, lift, and electrical standards under production pressure, not just in training.
  • Own the customer interaction — handle the door knock, scope confirmation, change orders, and on-site escalations. Never promise an unconfirmed return date — escalate to the TM/AM instead.
  • Control product use — place only the number of strands specified in the app to avoid over-installation; confirm with the crew leader before adding more and track the total installed.
  • Own the job data in the app — the ST inputs all job data — photos, video, strand and product counts, and completion — and is accountable for it being correct. What the ST enters is what the office sees: it's the record job costing, the yearly product order, and pay all run on, so getting it right is the ST's responsibility, not something the office has to chase down.
  • Lead the strike — remove the crew's installed displays in Strike season and feed clean returns back to the warehouse.
  • Train the next crew — mentor JTs during their final crew phase; this is the ST's core multiplier function.

A day in the role

  1. Clock in at the warehouseArrive on time and clock in on the Deputy app. Lateness is treated as a real problem — it shifts the whole crew's day.
  2. Prep the truckClean the cab and bed, clear out the prior day's trash and unused product, and load the day's material against the schedule.
  3. Drive to the first job & walk itLead the crew walkthrough on arrival, set the plan, and brief each JT on their piece.
  4. InstallDirect the crew through rooflines, trees, stakes, strands, spheres, wreaths, bows, garland, and timers — making routing calls and metering strand counts to avoid over-installation.
  5. Document & close out the jobCapture photos, video, and counts; confirm scope with the customer; record completion in the job-management app. The ST owns the accuracy of everything entered — the office works off this record, not a second source.

No two days look alike — rooflines one day, a full day in a tree the next, a mix of every product the day after — but the leadership wrapper around the day stays constant. In Strike season the same daily rhythm runs in reverse: the crew removes displays and returns product instead of installing it.

Purpose of the role

The JT is the hands on the install — entry-level labor that executes the design under the crew leader's direction, one jobsite at a time.

Junior Tech is the highest-volume seasonal hire at Brite Nites and the front door to the field. No prior experience is assumed; the role is built to be trained from scratch through a phased program before a JT is cleared to work live sites. Repair Tech (RT)

The solo field specialist who keeps displays lit through the season — diagnosing and rebuilding failed installations one client at a time on a pre-planned route, with same-day overrides when safety or a customer escalation demands it.

TypeSeasonal (Sep–Mar)
Reports to / coordinates withArea Manager (daily)
Direct reportsNone
StaffingTier 1 territories only — 1 per territory
Trained toFull installer competency + diagnostics

Purpose of the role

The swiss army knife of the field — you can't fix what you can't build, so the RT is trained to rebuild every install type, then layers diagnostics, QC, and customer communication on top.

When a display fails after install, Brite Nites doesn't send a full crew for a single callback — it sends the Repair Tech. The RT runs a route of isolated repairs across the territory: a dead roofline section, a slipped clip, a tree strand out, a tripped GFI, a timer that won't fire. Repair is targeted re-installation — the RT diagnoses the failure, then rebuilds the affected section to the same standard as the original install.

The RT is also the most customer-facing solo operator in the field. They arrive alone at a client's home to fix something that's broken — the client is frustrated before the truck pulls up, and the quality of the fix is judged in the moment, by one person. Professional posture and judgment under pressure are core requirements, not nice-to-haves.

Between repairs, the RT runs quality-control checks on completed installs — every RT is QC-trained, which also sharpens diagnostic instinct: you learn what breaks by seeing what's installed poorly.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Coordinates daily with the Area Manager (AM). The AM is the RT's daily co-pilot — primary trainer, assigner of appointments, and the 9am review partner who carries client history and territory priorities the RT can't see.
  • Hired and gated by the Area Manager (AM) — runs MEWP certification and owns territory-level repair metrics. (A "Territory Manager" is the same AM seat covering a larger area.)
  • No direct reports. The RT works solo; on high-volume peak weeks a helper (often a Warehouse Associate on a split schedule) may be paired in informally — there is no formal "RT helper" role.
  • Tier 1 only. A dedicated seat staffed in larger territories; smaller markets handle repairs informally through the AM and STs.

The handbook does not, however, cite a national repair average under 18 hours or a set evening-coverage standard — it describes flexible territory repair windows that can shift the start/end by an hour or two. It also treats the Territory Manager as a separate seat above the Area Manager.

Compensation & engagement

  • Seasonal role — hired in early September, released after strike (roughly April–August off). Off-season retention happens only in rare, exceptional circumstances.
  • First-hire is the primary path. Unlike the Senior Tech, the RT does not require prior Brite experience — the right profile (reliable, efficient, comfortable with customers) is trained from scratch to full installer competency.
  • Season-over-season rehire runs through the abbreviated Returning-RT refresher (~1 week) rather than a full re-train.
  • Pay structure follows the seasonal field model; specific figures are set by the territory and not part of this profile.

Core responsibilities

  • Diagnose and repair failed displays — confirm the reported issue, catch adjacent defects, and rebuild the affected section to install standard. Roofline (wind/clip/bulb/water) is the most common category; tree & greenery second; hanging-light, specialty, and electrical faults follow.
  • Run a daily repair route — 4–8 stops across the territory, ordered for efficiency around committed client windows, executed solo.
  • Turn repairs around same-day — same-day service is the standard, not the exception: Brite's national repair average runs under 18 hours. Because displays fail and clients call once it's dark, the RT covers into the evening — often until ~9pm — to clear urgent fixes the day they come in.
  • Communicate professionally at every door — clean arrival, blame-neutral damage language, a clear timeline within the first two minutes, and a visible walkthrough at sign-off. No upselling, no lingering.
  • Document every visit — submit a service report (with photos for major fixes) in BriteNow before leaving the property, within 60 minutes of the stop.
  • Run quality-control checks between repairs on completed installs — flag defects to the AM, fix minor issues on the spot, schedule a return for major ones.
  • Handle additional installs during peak — greenery and lighting add-ons, and crew/commercial support as needed.
  • Cover the service period solo (mid-Dec–early Jan) — the primary point of contact when holiday lights fail; fast turnaround protects the client base.
  • Join the warehouse crew during strike — solo takedowns and processing returned material (sorting restockable / broken / discard) alongside the warehouse team.
  • Escalate by the rule, not by judgment — two-person jobs, structural damage, panel/main-line electrical work, confrontational clients, and over-window repairs all go to the AM. Work beyond strand / plug / GFI-outlet level is always licensed-electrician scope.

A day in the role

A repair day is appointment-driven, not site-driven — many clients, many problems, many fixes. It runs on a five-stage rhythm anchored by the AM touchpoint — and same-day calls reshape it constantly: when a display goes dark in the evening, the RT may run later (toward ~9pm) to clear it that night.

  1. 8:30 AM — Pre-review setupArrive at the warehouse, walk-around the truck, pull the day's ticket queue in BriteNow, and flag anything unusual for the AM.
  2. ~9:00 AM — AM review (15–20 min)The highest-leverage 20 minutes of the day. Priorities, per-appointment client history, parts needed, the weather call, and yesterday's open follow-ups. In peak, the day starts earlier — closer to 7am — to fit more stops.
  3. 9:20 AM — Routing & loadingPlan the most efficient route around committed windows; load the standing kit plus the day's specific parts. Don't depart without the parts identified in review — missing parts mean a second trip.
  4. 10:00 AM onward — Execution loopPer stop: arrive & introduce → communicate & set timeline → diagnose → repair → document in BriteNow before leaving → next. Call the AM from site if any stop runs past 2× its expected time. On same-day-call days the loop runs into the evening rather than ending mid-afternoon.
  5. Late afternoon / evening — Wrap & reportStage the truck for tomorrow, submit any outstanding service reports, flag restock needs, and route schedule-return follow-ups to the AM (the RT does not self-schedule returns). On a quiet day this lands mid-afternoon; on a heavy same-day-call day it's after the last evening fix — coverage runs to ~9pm.

Purpose of the role

The QP is an inspector, not an installer — it grades work that's already complete and turns "looks done" into measurable accountability.

Brite Nites installs thousands of designs a season, and the QP exists to verify each one was built correctly. The QP arrives at a completed job, walks it against the design in the app, fixes small things on the spot, and assigns the crew a 1–5 quality score. Those scores are the company's quality signal — they feed crew accountability and the end-of-season strike audit, and they bear on each Senior Tech's eligibility for production pay.

The QP carries no install stations of its own. Its targets are speed and honesty: a quality check ideally lands within 48 hours of install once the territory is caught up. Early in the season, before a QP is ramped, the AM performs the quality checks; once the QP is up to speed, that work shifts over.

The QP is also the "face of Brite Nites" at the home — well-groomed, professional, and trusted by the client. It is the seat that asks for the 5-star review and leaves the property cleaner than the crew found it.

  • Inspection standards are owned by the Area Manager — the territory needs one consistent quality bar; the QP applies it, it doesn't set it.
  • No direct reports. The QP scores the ST + JT crews but does not manage them.

Core responsibilities

  • Morning crew support. Get crews loaded and out the door fast — help pick extra material, manage the cage and track tools out (Utah), minimize time at the warehouse.
  • Returns processing (install season, daily). Sort what crews drop off by product type, re-roll strands with proper hand-over-hand technique (never rip a tangle — the #1 damage source), inspect for cuts/missing plugs/exposed copper, box used lights (40 strands max, labeled), and restock accessories.
  • Strike recovery (high volume). Receive returned material in bulk, break down returned jobs, sort by product then by condition (good / damaged / scrap), report actual returned items + damage into the deal profile, and restock usable inventory. No per-job counting on return — the full inventory count happens at end of strike.
  • Outbound shipping. Freight (LTL via FreightCenter — palletize, weigh, measure, BOL) and Southwest Cargo (pull SKUs against Tony's packing list, fill the SWA slip, drive to cargo). Double-check the destination airport — a wrong airport is one of the costliest WA mistakes.
  • Inbound receiving (early season). Unload shipping containers (Utah, August) and LTL freight, sort by product type onto pallets (not by job), shrink-wrap, and shelve. Report damage and discrepancies to Tony.
  • Scrap routing. Keep C9 wire and mini wire separate (C9 prices higher), keep scrap metal out of regular trash, bring ID to the scrap yard, return receipts to Tony.
  • Holiday structures. Help install, dismantle, transport (26'/20' U-Haul), and store seasonal structures — label components, protect fragile parts, two-person minimum on large items.
  • Daily cleanup (non-negotiable, every season). Sweep floors, empty trash, clear trip hazards, return tools, secure the cage (Utah). Leaving the space right every day is a clearance criterion.
  • Errands. Home Depot runs (exact list, return receipts), deliveries, and Noel Decor heavy lifting only — WAs never pull or pack Noel Decor orders, and never hand off a company gas/credit card.

Purpose of the role

Roll the lights well and the install goes fast and clean — the bundle you hand off is the first step of every job.

The Light Roller is a hands-on warehouse contractor who turns last season's tangled light bags back into tight, tested, install-ready bundles. It is a single-task, high-repetition role: untangle, roll, tie, test, sort, stack. During strike season, the same hands also help the warehouse crew process the strands coming back off houses.

Rolling quality has real downstream cost. A sloppy bundle tangles on the ladder — minutes per strand, hundreds of strands per job — and bad technique cracks bulbs and kinks wires, leaving dead spots on the roofline. Good rolling is quiet, fast, and consistent.

It is the most condensed seat in the org. There are no formal phases, quizzes, or certifications; the role is learned hands-on in a day and assessed by direct observation. Authority is narrow by design — the Light Roller rolls and stacks; counting, inventory, and audit stay with the Warehouse Associate.

Reporting & compensation

Where it sits

  • Sits at the entry of the warehouse line; the Warehouse Associate (WA) is the day-to-day trainer and quality check.
  • The Area Manager (AM) coordinates start dates, checks productivity, and escalates issues.
  • The Area Manager (AM) owns the hiring pipeline (Indeed posting, interviews, contractor agreements) and sets territory rolling targets.
  • No direct reports; no on-site supervisory authority.
  • Most active during strike season (Jan), with occasional pre-season prep where a rolling backlog justifies it.

Handbook note: In the current handbook the Territory Manager (a separate seat above the Area Manager) owns the Light Roller hiring pipeline and rolling targets, with the Warehouse Associate as the day-to-day trainer. This profile treats the Territory Manager as an Area Manager stretched over more ground and assigns that hiring ownership to the AM.

Compensation

  • Contractor (W-9), paid a piece rate per strand rolled — paid before taxes, responsible for their own.
  • Paid every payroll cycle through Bill.com (not Gusto); requires bank info and a signed contractor agreement.
  • Pay is tied to deliverable quality, not raw count — only bundles meeting the WA's standard (tightness, knot, testing, sorting) count toward the strand total. Failed bundles are rework, not billable output.
  • Self-paced and self-motivated by design — faster, cleaner rolling earns more.

Per-strand rates are set per season and tracked daily; exact figures are covered in the Light Roller Management training, not quoted here.

Core responsibilities

  • Untangle light strands from bulk bags without damaging bulbs or connectors.
  • Roll each strand using the pinch-and-alternate folding technique into a tight, even bundle.
  • Tie the knot — leave two light spaces at the end, wrap tight, and knot the male and female ends so the bundle survives boxing and transport.
  • Test every bundle on a power strip, discard broken strands, and keep only verified bundles.
  • Sort by color appearance — group aged bundles separately from fresh so trees light up symmetrically on-site.
  • Stack finished bundles in their designated bin so the WA can count them.
  • Help process returns alongside the warehouse crew during strike — strands coming back off houses get untangled, rolled, and re-tested.
  • Build speed toward the seasonal target while holding the quality bar.

Out of scope: counting, Monday.com updates, and inventory reconciliation are WA responsibilities. Light Rollers do not work at height, on roofs, or with electrical systems — ladder use is limited to reaching high shelves.

The rolling cycle

Learned hands-on in a single day with the WA, who demonstrates the full cycle once, then watches and corrects each step until the motion is smooth.

  1. UntangleEmpty a bag onto the floor and separate strands — firm enough to be quick, gentle enough not to crack bulbs.
  2. RollStart at the male end; pinch every other bulb to alternate top and bottom of the hand, building a tight bundle. This is where most practice time goes.
  3. Tie the knotLeave two light spaces, wrap the bundle tight, and tie the male and female ends so it won't come undone in the box.
  4. TestPlug the male end into a power strip and verify every bulb lights. Discard broken strands; keep the good ones.
  5. Sort & stackGroup bundles by color appearance and stack them in the personal bin for the WA's end-of-shift count.
